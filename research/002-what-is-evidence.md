# Literature Review: What is Evidence?

## 1. Philosophy

### Definition
In philosophy, evidence is generally understood as that which **supports or justifies belief**. The philosophical tradition treats evidence as a relationship between propositional content and some ground or warrant. Key distinctions:
- **Propositional evidence**: Evidence that something is the case (proposition p is supported by evidence)
- **Evidentialism**: The view that one is justified in believing p iff one has evidence that supports p

### Key Concepts
- **Justification**: The warrant or reason that makes belief rational
- **Support relation**: The logical or probabilistic relationship between evidence and claim
- **Evidential base**: The totality of evidence available to a reasoner
- **Internalism**: Evidence is internally accessible to the knower
- **Externalism**: Evidence may include factors not accessible to consciousness

### Assumptions
- Evidence is truth-conducive (supports true beliefs)
- The support relation can be analyzed
- Evidence is distinguishable from non-evidentiary factors (prejudice, bias)
- Some evidence is better than other evidence (comparability)

### Strengths
- Provides rigorous analysis of the evidence-belief relationship
- Addresses fundamental questions about epistemic justification
- Distinguishes evidence from mere belief or assertion

### Limitations
- Often abstract and disconnected from practical application
- Difficult to operationalize for specific cases
- Unclear how evidence relates to non-propositional knowledge (know-how)

### Relevance to Engineering
- Philosophy provides the foundational framework for thinking about evidence
- The support relation analysis applies to engineering claims and justifications
- Questions of internal vs. external evidence relate to engineering documentation

## 2. Epistemology

### Definition
Epistemology treats evidence as the basis for knowledge and justified belief. Contemporary epistemology debates:
- **Foundationalism**: Some evidence is self-justifying (basic beliefs)
- **Coherentism**: Evidence is justified by its coherence with other beliefs
- **Infinitism**: Evidence chains can go on infinitely without circularity being problematic

### Key Concepts
- **Evidential support**: How evidence E supports hypothesis H
- **Probability**: Evidential relationships analyzed probabilistically (Bayesian epistemology)
- ** testimony**: Evidence from others' reports and assertions
- **Experience**: Evidence from perception and memory
- **Induction**: Evidence-based reasoning from particulars to universals

### Assumptions
- Evidence can be weighed and compared
- Some evidence is more powerful than other evidence
- Evidence relationships follow patterns (logical, probabilistic, or causal)
- Experience provides genuine evidence about the world

### Strengths
- Rigorous formal frameworks (probability theory, logic)
- Addresses the relationship between evidence and theory
- Distinguishes good from bad evidence practices

### Limitations
- Formal frameworks may not capture intuitive evidential judgments
- Difficult to specify when evidence is "sufficient"
- May not handle novel or unprecedented situations well

### Relevance to Engineering
- Bayesian approaches useful for engineering decision-making under uncertainty
- Testimony evidence relevant for engineering standards and expertise
- Foundational vs. coherentist debates inform how engineering knowledge is structured

## 3. Science

### Definition
Scientific evidence is empirical data that supports or refutes scientific claims. The scientific method defines evidence through:
- **Empiricism**: Evidence comes from observation and experiment
- **Reproducibility**: Evidence must be independently verifiable
- **Falsifiability**: Evidence can potentially refute, not just confirm (Popper)
- **Peer review**: Evidence is evaluated by expert communities

### Key Concepts
- **Data**: Raw observations and measurements
- **Results**: Processed data presented as evidence
- **Falsification**: Evidence that disconfirms a hypothesis
- **Replication**: Independent reproduction of findings
- **Effect size**: The magnitude of an observed phenomenon
- **Statistical significance**: The probability of observed results occurring by chance

### Assumptions
- The natural world provides reliable evidence through observation
- Measurement instruments can accurately detect phenomena
- Statistical methods can distinguish signal from noise
- Reproducibility indicates genuine phenomena

### Strengths
- Highly systematic and documented evidence practices
- Built-in mechanisms for self-correction (replication, peer review)
- Quantitative rigor through statistical methods
- Produces reliable, actionable evidence about natural phenomena

### Limitations
- Limited to phenomena measurable and reproducible
- Observer effects can influence measurements
- Statistical significance ≠ practical significance
- Publication bias affects the evidence base

### Relevance to Engineering
- Scientific evidence practices inform engineering experimental methods
- Reproducibility concerns apply to engineering testing and validation
- Statistical methods directly applicable to engineering data analysis

## 4. Engineering

### Definition
Engineering does not have a unified theory of evidence. Evidence in engineering is characteristically **practical and outcome-oriented**:
- **Works**: The artifact functions as designed
- **Tests pass**: Formal tests demonstrate compliance
- **History**: Past successful use indicates reliability
- **Expert judgment**: Experienced engineers assess evidence qualitatively
- **Simulation**: Computational models provide evidential data

### Key Concepts
- **Verification**: Does the artifact meet specifications?
- **Validation**: Does the artifact meet real-world needs?
- **Testing**: Systematic examination of behavior
- **Failure analysis**: Evidence from what went wrong
- **Prototyping**: Evidence from iterative physical or computational models
- **Standards**: Codified evidence requirements from authoritative bodies

### Assumptions
- Direct observation of functioning artifacts provides evidence
- Past performance indicates future reliability (with caveats)
- Testing can reveal relevant evidence about system behavior
- Engineering judgment can evaluate evidence in novel situations

### Strengths
- Highly pragmatic approach to evidence
- Emphasis on demonstration through functioning artifacts
- Incorporates multiple evidence types (test, history, judgment)
- Real-world validation is ultimate criterion

### Limitations
- Often relies on informal, undocumented evidence
- Testing can only demonstrate presence, not absence of defects
- Expert judgment is difficult to formalize or transfer
- May undervalue evidence that doesn't fit traditional categories

### Relevance to Engineering
- Engineering evidence practices are the most directly relevant to KDE
- Verification/validation distinction crucial for engineering knowledge
- Integration of formal testing with informal judgment is characteristic

## 5. Law

### Definition
Legal evidence encompasses everything presented in court to determine facts. The law has developed elaborate rules governing evidence:
- **Admissibility**: Rules determine what evidence can be presented
- **Burden of proof**: Who must provide evidence and at what standard
- **Preponderance**: More likely than not (>50%)
- **Beyond reasonable doubt**: High certainty required for criminal cases
- **Direct vs. circumstantial**: Direct observation vs. inference from circumstances

### Key Concepts
- **Testimony**: Witnesses' statements under oath
- **Documentary evidence**: Physical records and documents
- **Physical evidence**: Tangible objects presented as evidence
- **Hearsay**: Out-of-court statements offered for truth (generally inadmissible)
- **Chain of custody**: Documentation of evidence handling
- **Exhibits**: Physical evidence presented in court

### Assumptions
- Fact-finders (judges, juries) can evaluate evidence fairly
- Procedural rules help ensure reliable fact-finding
- Testimony from credible witnesses constitutes genuine evidence
- Physical evidence provides objective facts

### Strengths
- Highly developed system for handling conflicting evidence
- Sophisticated rules for weighing testimony credibility
- Clear standards for burden of proof
- Emphasis on procedure and documentation

### Limitations
- Designed for legal fact-finding, not scientific inquiry
- Rules of admissibility may exclude relevant evidence
- Adversarial system may distort evidence presentation
- Historical practices (e.g., exclusionary rule) prioritize process over truth

### Relevance to Engineering
- Preponderance vs. beyond-reasonable-doubt parallels engineering risk levels
- Chain-of-custody concept applies to engineering evidence provenance
- Hearsay rules illuminate the value of direct vs. indirect evidence

## 6. Information Science

### Definition
Information science treats evidence primarily as **information content** or **informational relevance**:
- **Evidence as information**: Evidence is a particular type of information that supports conclusions
- **Relevance**: Evidence is information relevant to a query or question
- **Information retrieval**: Systems retrieve evidence relevant to information needs
- **Evidence-based practice**: Systematic retrieval and use of research evidence

### Key Concepts
- **Relevance feedback**: Users indicate which retrieved items are evidence
- **Information need**: The question to which evidence is sought
- **Evidence synthesis**: Combining multiple sources of evidence
- **Bibliometrics**: Quantitative analysis of evidence sources
- **Systematic review**: Structured evidence identification and appraisal

### Assumptions
- Evidence can be retrieved, stored, and managed as information
- Relevance is a stable property of information items
- Systematic processes can identify comprehensive evidence
- Quality indicators (citation count, journal impact) indicate evidence strength

### Strengths
- Systematic approaches to evidence retrieval
- Tools and systems for evidence management
- Structured synthesis methods (systematic reviews)
- Bridges evidence and decision-making

### Limitations
- May reduce evidence to text-accessible content
- Relevance judgments are subjective and variable
- Quality indicators imperfect proxies for evidence quality
- May miss non-textual or tacit evidence

### Relevance to Engineering
- Evidence retrieval systems applicable to engineering knowledge bases
- Systematic review methods useful for engineering literature review
- Information relevance concepts inform evidence prioritization

## 7. Artificial Intelligence

### Definition
AI treats evidence as **data that supports conclusions** in computational systems:
- **Evidence as features**: Input data that machine learning models use
- **Probabilistic evidence**: Data that updates probability distributions (Bayesian networks)
- **Knowledge graphs**: Entities and relationships as evidential structure
- **Explanation evidence**: Evidence that supports AI explanations
- **Ground truth**: Labeled data used as evidence for training

### Key Concepts
- **Training data**: Examples from which models derive patterns
- **Feature engineering**: Selecting relevant data as evidence
- **Confidence scores**: Probabilistic evidence for model outputs
- **Adversarial examples**: Evidence that fools models unexpectedly
- **Explainable AI**: Evidence supporting model decisions
- **Ground truth**: Reference data against which outputs are evaluated

### Assumptions
- Patterns in data constitute genuine evidence
- Large datasets improve evidence quality
- Statistical patterns in training data generalize to new cases
- Ground truth data is accurate evidence

### Strengths
- Quantitative, scalable evidence processing
- Probabilistic frameworks for evidence combination
- Automated pattern discovery in large evidence sets
- Systematic documentation of evidence sources (training data)

### Limitations
- Correlation ≠ causation; patterns may not constitute genuine evidence
- Ground truth may be noisy or biased
- Models can learn spurious patterns that constitute "evidence" in training but not reality
- Black-box models may not reveal what evidence they use

### Relevance to Engineering
- Machine learning provides tools for engineering evidence analysis
- Ground truth concept parallels engineering validation data
- Explanation evidence needed for engineering AI systems

## 8. Knowledge Management

### Definition
Knowledge management treats evidence as **organizational information that supports decisions and actions**:
- **Best practices**: Documented evidence of effective approaches
- **Lessons learned**: Evidence from past projects
- **After-action reviews**: Structured evidence extraction from experiences
- **Communities of practice**: Social mechanisms for evidence sharing
- **Expert networks**: Connections to individuals with relevant evidence

### Key Concepts
- **Tacit evidence**: Evidence held by individuals but not documented
- **Explicit evidence**: Evidence codified in documents and systems
- **Knowledge capture**: Converting tacit evidence to explicit form
- **Storytelling**: Narrative sharing of evidence through examples
- **Evidence repositories**: Systems for storing and retrieving organizational evidence
- **Expertise location**: Finding people with relevant evidence

### Assumptions
- Past experience provides evidence for future decisions
- Documentation makes evidence more accessible and reusable
- Sharing evidence benefits organizational performance
- Evidence quality can be assessed and improved

### Strengths
- Focus on organizational evidence rather than individual knowledge
- Bridges tacit and explicit evidence
- Emphasizes evidence use, not just storage
- Social mechanisms for evidence validation

### Limitations
- Documentation may not capture full evidential context
- Tacit evidence remains difficult to capture
- Evidence may become outdated without maintenance
- Organizational politics can affect evidence sharing

### Relevance to Engineering
- Lessons learned and after-action reviews directly applicable to engineering
- Tacit/explicit evidence distinction crucial for engineering knowledge
- Expert networks provide evidence sources beyond documents

# Comparative Analysis

## Common Characteristics Across All Definitions

Every discipline, despite different emphases, identifies the following:

1. **Support relation** — Evidence is always *evidence for* something. Evidence without a claim is just data. Evidence gains meaning through its relationship to what it supports or refutes. This relationship can be logical, probabilistic, causal, or practical.

2. **Something beyond itself** — Evidence points beyond itself to something else. Evidence is not self-justifying; it derives its evidential force from its relationship to something it represents or indicates. This is true whether the relationship is correspondence (philosophy), correlation (AI), or demonstration (engineering).

3. **Assessable quality** — All disciplines recognize that evidence varies in quality. Some evidence is strong, some is weak. Some evidence clearly supports a claim, some merely suggests. Evidence is not binary (present/absent) but scalar (weak/strong).

4. **Context-dependent** — Evidence does not exist in isolation. What counts as evidence depends on the question, the domain, the available alternatives, and the standards of the field. Evidence evaluated as strong in one context may be weak in another.

5. **Transformable** — Evidence can be processed, combined, weighed, and transformed. Raw data becomes processed evidence. Multiple pieces of evidence combine to support or refute claims. Evidence can be synthesized, not just aggregated.

## Unique Characteristics by Discipline

| Characteristic | Disciplines | Implication |
|----------------|-------------|-------------|
| Truth-conduciveness | Philosophy, Science | Evidence must lead to truth (normative) |
| Reproducibility | Science, Engineering | Evidence must be independently verifiable |
| Admissibility | Law | Procedural rules govern evidence |
| Physicality | Law, Engineering | Tangible artifacts as evidence |
| Probabilistic | AI, Epistemology | Evidence updates probability distributions |
| Organizational | KM | Evidence exists in social/institutional contexts |
| Computational | AI, Information Science | Evidence processed by systems |
| Experiential | KM, Engineering | Experience constitutes evidence |

## Conflicting Assumptions

1. **Objectivity vs. Interpretivism**
   - Philosophy/Science: Evidence is objective; independent of the observer
   - Law/KM: Evidence is always presented, interpreted, and evaluated by people
   - This conflicts on whether evidence has existence independent of those who assess it

2. **Formality vs. Informality**
   - Law/Science: Evidence follows formal rules and procedures
   - Engineering/KM: Informal evidence (judgment, experience) is legitimate
   - This conflicts on what counts as acceptable evidence

3. **Completeness vs. Sufficiency**
   - Science: More evidence is generally better; completeness is ideal
   - Engineering/Law: Practical evidence sufficiency is acceptable; completeness is often impossible
   - This conflicts on whether evidence should be comprehensive

4. **Individual vs. Social**
   - Philosophy/AI: Evidence can be evaluated objectively by individuals
   - Law/KM: Evidence is fundamentally social; testimony is primary evidence
   - This conflicts on whether individual assessment or social processes determine evidence quality

5. **Static vs. Dynamic**
   - Philosophy/Science: Evidence is a static relationship between propositions
   - KM/Engineering: Evidence evolves with experience and new data
   - This conflicts on whether evidence is a state or a process

## Fundamental Concepts Across Disciplines

Regardless of theoretical orientation, these concepts appear essential:

1. **Support** — Evidence supports or refutes claims. The support relation is the core of evidential reasoning. Whether analyzed logically, probabilistically, or practically, evidence does something in relation to claims.

2. **Quality** — Evidence varies in strength. Some evidence is definitive, some merely suggestive. The quality of evidence determines how much weight it should receive in reasoning and decision-making.

3. **Retrieval** — Evidence must be accessible to be useful. Whether through documents, testimony, databases, or memory, evidence must be findable and retrievable when needed.

4. **Evaluation** — Someone must judge evidence quality. Whether judge, scientist, engineer, or AI system, the assessment of evidence requires an evaluator.

5. **Context** — Evidence is always evidence for something specific, in some situation. Evidence stripped of context loses its evidential force.

## Semantic vs. Substantive Disagreements

**Likely Semantic**:
- "Support" vs. "justify" vs. "warrant" vs. "confirm" — may describe the same support relationship with different implications
- "Data" vs. "evidence" vs. "information" — distinctions may be matters of degree, not kind
- "Strong evidence" vs. "convincing evidence" vs. "sufficient evidence" — may be context-dependent synonyms

**Likely Substantive**:
- Whether evidence must be truth-conducive (philosophy says yes; pragmatic views may disagree)
- Whether experience constitutes evidence (engineering says yes; formal epistemology may disagree)
- Whether procedural rules enhance or diminish evidence quality (law's adversarial vs. inquisitorial approaches)
- Whether evidence is fundamentally individual or social (affects how evidence should be evaluated)

# Synthesis

## What Should KDE Adopt?

1. **Multi-modal evidence concept**
   - Evidence is not only propositional data
   - Test results, demonstrations, expert judgment, and experience are all evidence
   - KDE should support diverse evidence types rather than privileging one

2. **Evidence-quality gradation**
   - Evidence is not binary (present/absent) but scalar (weak/strong)
   - KDE should enable quality assessment and weighting
   - Multiple weak evidence items can combine to be strong

3. **Context-dependence of evidence**
   - What counts as evidence depends on domain, question, and standards
   - KDE evidence practices must be situated within specific engineering contexts
   - Evidence portability across contexts requires explicit translation

4. **Evidence as relationship, not object**
   - Evidence is always evidence *for* something
   - Evidence cannot be evaluated in isolation from claims
   - KDE systems should emphasize evidence-claim relationships

5. **Practical sufficiency standards**
   - Engineering cannot wait for scientific completeness
   - Evidence should meet practical sufficiency for decisions
   - Different decisions may require different evidence thresholds

## What Should KDE Reject?

1. **Monist evidence ontology**
   - No single evidence type is universally superior
   - Reject privileging of scientific evidence over engineering evidence or vice versa
   - Each context has appropriate evidence types

2. **Completeness as ideal**
   - Evidence completeness is often impossible and always expensive
   - Reject the notion that good evidence requires comprehensive evidence
   - Sufficiency, not completeness, should be the standard

3. **Evidence/knowledge conflation**
   - Evidence is not knowledge
   - Evidence supports knowledge claims but is not itself knowledge
   - KDE should maintain clear evidence-knowledge distinction

4. **Static evidence**
   - Evidence is not fixed for all time
   - New evidence can modify or overturn previous evidence
   - KDE should enable evidence updating and revision

## What Assumptions Should Remain Open?

1. **Objectivity of evidence**
   - Whether evidence is objectively good or merely intersubjectively agreed as good
   - KDE can operate without resolving this philosophical debate

2. **Truth-conduciveness requirement**
   - Whether evidence must lead to truth or merely practical success
   - Engineering pragmatism may be compatible with either view

3. **Individual vs. social evidence**
   - Whether evidence evaluation is fundamentally individual or social
   - KDE can incorporate both individual expertise and social validation

4. **Tacit evidence legitimacy**
   - Whether tacit evidence (undocumented, inexpressible) is genuine evidence
   - Conservative approach: treat as evidence when accessible; remain agnostic on the rest

# Candidate Working Definition

## WORKING DEFINITION (Not Validated)

Based on the evidence and synthesis, the following is a single candidate working definition for KDE:

> **Engineering evidence is retrievable support, in context, that enables evaluation of knowledge claims.**

## Rationale

This definition emerges from the synthesis:

- **"Retrievable"** — Evidence must be accessible to be useful; evidence that cannot be found is ineffective
- **"Support"** — Evidence supports or refutes claims; the support relation is fundamental
- **"In context"** — Evidence is always situated; context determines what counts as evidence
- **"Enables evaluation"** — Evidence's purpose is to allow assessment of knowledge claims
- **"Knowledge claims"** — Evidence serves knowledge; the evidence-knowledge relationship is primary

## What This Definition Excludes

- Evidence evaluated in isolation from claims
- Evidence that cannot be retrieved or accessed
- Evidence that does not relate to knowledge claims
- Complete (as opposed to sufficient) evidence

## What This Definition Accommodates

- Multiple evidence modalities (test, judgment, experience, documentation)
- Evidence quality gradation (retrievability and relevance vary)
- Context-dependence (different contexts have different evidence)
- Tacit evidence (when accessible through expertise networks)

## Not a Final Definition

This is a **WORKING definition** that will be validated through KDE methodology before potential promotion to `/knowledge/`.

## Current Understanding

Evidence is a relational concept: evidence is always *evidence for* something. Across eight disciplines:

- **Support relationship**: Evidence supports or refutes claims, propositions, or actions
- **Quality gradation**: Evidence varies from weak to strong; context affects quality assessment
- **Multiple modalities**: Test results, testimony, documentation, experience, and simulation all constitute evidence
- **Context dependence**: What counts as evidence depends on domain, question, and standards
- **Retrievability**: Evidence must be accessible to be useful

No discipline has definitively solved "What is evidence?" The concept shares with "knowledge" a contested nature with practical utility across all domains.

## Counter Evidence
- Some definitions may over-generalize evidence beyond usefulness
- Quality gradation is difficult to operationalize
- Tacit evidence may be genuine but resist formal treatment
- Context-dependence makes evidence comparison across contexts problematic
